\chapter{INTRODUCTION}

\section{Problem Statement}

This thesis addresses the software-engineering problem of building an
interactive, real-time English speaking-practice system. From a system-design
perspective, such a system is challenging not because of any single feature but
because it must coordinate several subsystems that have very different timing and
reliability characteristics. A learner speaks into a browser, so the system must
capture audio on the client and transport it in real time to a server; it must
convert speech to text, generate conversational tutor replies quickly enough to
sustain an interaction, and then persist what happened in the session so that it
can be reviewed and graded afterwards. Recognition and reply generation belong on
a latency-sensitive path, whereas grading and analysis are heavier and can run
later, so the system has to keep these two concerns separate while still moving
data reliably from one to the other. The background technologies behind these
stages --- real-time communication, speech recognition, and conversational
language models --- are surveyed in Chapter~2.

The core difficulty is therefore one of integration and boundary management
rather than of any individual algorithm: a real-time transport path, a speech
recognition component, a conversational model, a durable data store, an
asynchronous grading stage, and a review interface must be composed into a single
coherent system with clear responsibilities and well-defined hand-offs.

Two cross-cutting concerns sharpen this difficulty. The first is reliability at
the boundary between the live session and the deferred grading: when a session
ends, the system must record what happened durably and then move a unit of work
to the grading stage, without losing that work if the messaging step does not
succeed. The second is privacy: because the system processes a learner's speech,
the recognized transcript is sensitive, and care is needed so that raw transcript
text does not leak into operational logs. These concerns motivate specific design
choices --- a durable hand-off recorded in the database and a scoped redaction of
transcript text from the relevant recognition log lines --- that are developed in
the later chapters. This thesis approaches the overall problem as a
software/system-design exercise and builds LUVE, a controlled-demo prototype, to
demonstrate one coherent way of structuring it. The thesis does not study the educational effectiveness of such a system, nor
does it make any claim about market demand or learning improvement; its concern is
the design and implementation of the software.

\section{Research Objectives}
\subsection{General Objective}

The general objective is to design and implement LUVE as a coherent software
system for real-time English speaking practice, delivered as a controlled-demo
research prototype rather than a production-ready product. The emphasis is on the
architecture, the boundaries between components, the data and control flows, and
the runtime integration that allow the prototype to operate end to end.

\subsection{Specific Objectives}

The specific objectives are the following:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item to design a multi-component architecture for real-time speaking
  interaction, with explicit component boundaries;
  \item to implement a controlled-demo prototype integrating the frontend
  client, the Core API, the TEN/WebRTC gateway, the STT component, the real-time
  LLM tutor reply path, PostgreSQL, RabbitMQ, and the grading-worker;
  \item to separate the real-time interaction from asynchronous grading, so that
  the latency-sensitive path is not burdened by analysis work;
  \item to persist completed sessions and support saved-session review; and
  \item to document the reliability and privacy boundaries of the system,
  including the scoped redaction of STT transcript text from the relevant
  recognition log lines and the durable \texttt{session\_outbox} hand-off.
\end{itemize}

These objectives are deliberately framed as software objectives. The thesis does
not aim to demonstrate production readiness.

\section{Research Subject and Scope}
\subsection{Research Subject}

The subject of the thesis is the LUVE software system itself --- its
architecture, its constituent components (the frontend client, the Core API, the
TEN/WebRTC gateway, the STT module, the real-time LLM tutor, PostgreSQL,
RabbitMQ, the \texttt{session\_outbox} table, and the grading-worker), and the
data and control flows that connect them. The single reference point for every
claim in this thesis is the frozen source-code baseline \texttt{main@6a61bc8}.

\subsection{Research Scope}

The scope is intentionally bounded. LUVE is studied as a controlled-demo,
research prototype, not as a production product. Speech recognition is treated in
its stable demo mode only: the \texttt{small.en} model in \texttt{forced\_en}
mode with second-pass verification disabled, which makes recognition
English-focused; stable multilingual recognition is outside the scope and is not
claimed. The pronunciation value reported by the system is treated as a clarity
estimate derived from speech-recognition confidence and uncertainty, not as
acoustic or phoneme-level pronunciation scoring. Grading is considered in both of
its configured modes: an offline development-preview (``fake'') grader by default
and an optional real LLM-based grader enabled by configuration. On the
reliability side, the outbox relay is treated as disabled by default, so recovery
from a failed publish is manual unless the relay is explicitly enabled, and the
hand-off is at-least-once-oriented rather than exactly-once. Horizontal scaling is
not within scope and is not validated. Finally, the unmerged ``cat companion''
interface (PR~\#2) is not part of the baseline and is excluded from the scope of
this thesis.

\section{Research Methodology}

The work followed a sequence of system-design and implementation steps. It began
with requirements and boundary analysis, separating the functional needs ---
real-time speaking practice, recognition, tutor replies, session persistence, and
grading of saved sessions --- from the non-functional concerns of privacy,
security, and reliability. This was followed by architecture design, in which the
components and their boundaries were defined, and by database and outbox design,
in which durable state and the completion-to-grading hand-off were modeled around
PostgreSQL and the \texttt{session\_outbox} table. The design was then mapped onto
the implementation across the frontend, the Core API, the real-time gateway, the
STT and tutor paths, and the grading-worker. Verification was carried out at a
level appropriate to a controlled demo, combining build and static checks,
targeted backend checks, and a runtime smoke and pre-demo verification checklist;
checklist items count as recorded results only when executed in the target
environment. Throughout, the limitations of the prototype were documented
truthfully rather than glossed over. The methodology did not include user
studies, controlled experiments, benchmark measurements, or a
continuous-integration pipeline.

\section{Scientific and Practical Significance}

The contributions of this thesis are technical and system-oriented, and are
treated in detail in Chapter~5. In brief, the thesis contributes a componentized
LUVE architecture with explicit boundaries; a clean separation of the real-time
speaking interaction from asynchronous grading; a persistence and hand-off design
built on PostgreSQL, RabbitMQ, and the durable \texttt{session\_outbox} table;
explicit and honest boundaries around where artificial intelligence is used,
including the distinction between real-time tutor replies and grading and the
clarity-estimate treatment of pronunciation; and a controlled-demo DevOps and
documentation posture in which the prototype's scope and limitations are stated
plainly. These are offered as software-design results rather than as claims about
learning outcomes or measured performance.

\section{Thesis Structure}

The remainder of the thesis is organized as follows. Chapter~1 (this chapter)
introduces the problem, objectives, scope, methodology, and contributions.
Chapter~2 reviews the background and the related technologies on which LUVE is
built. Chapter~3 presents the system design, covering the architecture, the

design. Chapter~4 describes the development environment, the implementation

% ------------------- CHAPTER 2 ------------------------------------
\chapter{THEORETICAL BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK}

This chapter reviews the technical background and the related technologies on
which LUVE is built. Consistent with the software/system-design focus of the
thesis, the goal is to introduce the building blocks of a real-time
speaking-practice system --- real-time communication, speech recognition,
conversational language models, asynchronous messaging, relational persistence
with a transactional outbox, and web-application architecture --- rather than to
survey the educational literature. Each technology is described at the level
needed to motivate the design decisions presented in Chapter~3.

\section{Overview of Real-Time Speaking Practice Systems}

Automated, computer-assisted speaking-practice systems that let a learner hold a
dialogue with a computer have been studied as dialogue-based computer-assisted
language learning~\cite{bibauwDialogueCALL2019}. A real-time speaking-practice
system is, in software terms, a pipeline that turns a learner's spoken input into
an interactive response and a reviewable record. Technically, such a system must
capture audio in the browser, transport
it to a server with low latency, convert the speech into text, generate a
dialogue response from that text, and persist the session so that it can be
reviewed and analyzed afterwards. Each of these stages is a distinct concern
with its own latency and reliability requirements, and the overall behavior
emerges from how they are composed rather than from any single stage. A natural
architectural split therefore arises between a latency-sensitive interactive
path --- capture, transport, recognition, and response --- and a slower,
deferred path that handles persistence-derived analysis after a session ends.
LUVE follows this general shape; the remainder of this chapter introduces the
specific technologies it uses for each concern. This thesis treats such systems
purely as software artifacts and makes no claim about their effect on learning
outcomes.

\section{Web-Based Real-Time Communication}

Because the learner interacts through a browser, the system needs a
communication layer capable of carrying audio in real time rather than through
ordinary request/response calls. Standard HTTP request/response interactions are
well suited to discrete operations such as authentication or fetching saved
data, but they are not designed for the continuous, low-latency, bidirectional
media flow that a live speaking interaction requires. Web Real-Time Communication
(WebRTC)~\cite{webrtcSpec} is the browser technology that addresses this need: it provides
peer-connection establishment, session negotiation, and the transport of media
and control data between a browser and a remote endpoint. At a high level,
WebRTC allows the client and the server-side gateway to negotiate a session and
then exchange real-time audio and control messages over that connection.

At a high level, establishing a WebRTC session involves negotiating the
parameters of the connection between the two endpoints and then establishing a
usable network path so that media can flow. The negotiation exchanges a
description of each side's media capabilities, and a connectivity-establishment
phase determines a route between the endpoints; once the connection is in place,
audio can be streamed continuously while additional control messages are
exchanged alongside it. The property that matters for this thesis is not the
detail of these phases but the capability they provide: a persistent,
low-latency channel between the browser and a server-side endpoint that is
fundamentally different from a sequence of independent HTTP requests. In
LUVE, this real-time layer is the boundary between the browser client and the
TEN/WebRTC gateway, and it carries the audio and control flow of a speaking
session; the heavier, non-interactive operations remain on the HTTP REST surface.
This separation of a real-time media boundary from conventional request/response
boundaries is a recurring theme in the design.

\section{Speech-to-Text Technology}

Speech-to-text (STT) is the component that converts the learner's spoken audio
into text that downstream components can process. The quality and timing of this
conversion shape everything that follows, since both the conversational response
and any later analysis operate on the recognized text and on the confidence
signals that accompany it. LUVE uses the \texttt{faster-whisper} implementation
of the Whisper family of speech-recognition models~\cite{radfordWhisper,fasterWhisper}.
In its stable demo
configuration, recognition runs with the \texttt{small.en} model in
\texttt{forced\_en} mode with second-pass verification disabled, which makes the
recognition path English-focused. This configuration is a deliberate scoping
choice for the prototype: stable multilingual recognition is not within scope and
is not claimed. The STT component produces recognized text together with
confidence and uncertainty signals; these signals are recognition-level
indicators that later inform grading, and they are explicitly not an acoustic or
phoneme-level pronunciation analysis. The distinction matters for the design:
confidence-derived signals can support a coarse, text-level estimate of how
clearly a learner was understood, but they do not measure articulation of
individual sounds, and the system is careful not to present them as if they did.
This thesis does not report recognition accuracy figures for the model.

\section{Large Language Models for Conversational Response}

Large language models (LLMs) are sequence models capable of generating fluent
natural-language text conditioned on an input~\cite{brownGPT3}. In LUVE, an LLM is used at the
architecture level to generate conversational tutor replies from the recognized
text during a session, so that the interaction can continue naturally. The
important point for the design is the boundary around this use: the real-time
LLM tutor reply path is responsible only for producing conversational replies and
is deliberately kept separate from the grading and scoring stage. Because such a
model generates text conditioned on its input, the same family of model can in
principle be applied either to produce a reply in the moment or, separately, to
help analyze a completed session; LUVE keeps these two applications in distinct
components with distinct timing, so that the conversational use never blocks on
or is conflated with the analytical use. Full
grading/scoring analysis is not performed on the real-time path; it is delegated
to the asynchronous grading-worker after a session completes. This thesis makes
no claim about the pedagogical effectiveness of the generated replies and reports
no performance figures for any specific model; the LLM is treated as a component
with a defined responsibility and a defined boundary within the system.

\section{Asynchronous Processing and Message Queues}

Not all of the system's work belongs on the real-time path. Grading and analysis
of a completed session are comparatively heavy and do not need to run while the
learner is speaking, so it is desirable to move them off the latency-sensitive
hot path and process them asynchronously. A message queue is the standard
mechanism for this kind of decoupling: a producer hands off a unit of work, and a
separate consumer processes it independently, so that the producer is not blocked
by the consumer's workload~\cite{rabbitmqDocs}. Beyond decoupling, message brokers commonly provide
useful operational properties: a consumer acknowledges a work item once it has
been processed, items that cannot be processed can be routed aside for inspection
rather than lost, and producers and consumers can be scaled or restarted
independently. These properties make a queue a natural fit for work that is
important but does not have to be performed synchronously with a user
interaction. LUVE uses RabbitMQ as the asynchronous channel that carries grading
work from the point of session completion to the grading-worker.
Consistent with the system's reliability design, after the database commit an
inline publish of the work item to RabbitMQ is attempted; this publish is not
treated as guaranteed, and the design therefore does not assume that delivery
always succeeds on the first attempt. Delivery is at-least-once-oriented with
idempotent, session-level processing rather than exactly-once, so that a work
item processed more than once does not produce duplicate results.

\section{Relational Persistence and Transactional Outbox}

A speaking-practice system needs durable storage for structured data --- accounts,
sessions, and grading outcomes --- with well-defined relationships and
transactional guarantees. A relational database such as PostgreSQL~\cite{postgresDocs} is a natural
fit, because it provides both the relational model and the ability to commit
several related changes atomically. That atomicity is central to a recurring
reliability problem: when a session completes, the system must both record the
completion in the database and arrange for a grading message to be sent, and a
naive implementation that writes to the database and then publishes to a separate
broker can fail between the two steps. This is the classic dual-write problem:
two independent systems --- the database and the message broker --- must both be
updated, but there is no single transaction spanning them, so a crash or a failed
publish after the database commit can leave the state change recorded without the
corresponding message ever being sent. The transactional outbox
pattern~\cite{transactionalOutbox} addresses
this by writing the outbound intent into the same database, in the same
transaction as the state change, so that the intent is durable if and only if the
state change is. A separate relay process can then read pending outbox entries
and publish them to the broker, retrying as needed, while consumers remain
idempotent so that a re-published item is harmless. In LUVE, the \texttt{session\_outbox} is an ordinary PostgreSQL
table rather than a queue service; the session state and the outbox intent are
committed together, and a relay would later deliver pending rows. In the baseline
that relay is disabled by default, so recovery from a failed publish is manual
unless the relay is explicitly enabled.

\section{Web Application Architecture and Deployment}

LUVE is structured following a common web-application pattern: a single-page
frontend, a backend that exposes an application programming interface, and
separate worker and infrastructure components. In this pattern, the browser
runs a rich client that renders the interface and calls backend endpoints, the
backend concentrates application logic behind an API, and long-running or
deferred work is handled by separate worker processes rather than inside the
request path. Such a separation keeps each tier focused on a single concern and
allows the interactive surface, the API, and the background work to evolve and
fail independently. In LUVE, the frontend is a single-page application built with
React, Vite, and TypeScript; the backend is implemented in Python with the
FastAPI framework; durable data lives in PostgreSQL; RabbitMQ provides
asynchronous messaging; and a separate worker process performs grading. Rather
than being deployed as a standalone service, the frontend is built into static
assets that the backend serves. These components are packaged and coordinated for
local, demonstration use with Docker Compose, which allows the infrastructure
services and the application services to be brought up
together~\cite{fastapiDocs,reactDocs,viteDocs,dockerComposeDocs,postgresDocs,rabbitmqDocs}.
This thesis names these technologies only as
the components used in LUVE and does not assert specific version numbers or any
claim of production readiness; the deployment is a controlled-demo packaging
rather than a hosted, production-grade service.

\section{Chapter Summary}

This chapter introduced the technologies underlying LUVE: WebRTC for real-time
browser-to-server audio and control; \texttt{faster-whisper}/Whisper for
English-focused speech-to-text in the stable demo mode; large language models for
generating conversational tutor replies on a path kept separate from grading;
RabbitMQ for asynchronous grading work with at-least-once-oriented, non-guaranteed
delivery; PostgreSQL with a transactional outbox (\texttt{session\_outbox}) for
durable persistence and a reliable completion-to-grading hand-off; and a
single-page frontend with a FastAPI backend, a grading worker, and Docker Compose
for controlled-demo packaging. Together, these technologies and the boundaries
between them motivate the system design presented in Chapter~3, where they are
composed into the LUVE architecture, database design, interface design, and
processing flows.

\chapter{RESEARCH METHODOLOGY AND SYSTEM DESIGN}

\section{Research Methodology}

This chapter follows a system-design methodology: rather than evaluating the
system empirically, it derives a design from the requirements of the prototype
and presents that design at the level of components, boundaries, data, and
control flow. The methodology proceeds in stages that mirror the structure of
the chapter. It begins by deriving requirements from the LUVE controlled-demo
goal --- a real-time English speaking-practice prototype --- and separating the
functional needs from the non-functional and design concerns. From these
requirements it identifies the component boundaries of the system, so that each
component has a single, well-defined responsibility and a clear interface to the
others. A central design decision follows from this step: the real-time
interaction is separated from asynchronous grading, so that the
latency-sensitive path is not burdened by heavier analysis work. The methodology
then designs the persistence and the completion-to-grading hand-off, modeling
durable state and the outbox intent around a relational database, and afterwards
designs the user interface and the processing flows that tie the components
together end to end. At each stage, the limitations and scope of the prototype
are kept explicit rather than deferred, so that the design is presented honestly
within its controlled-demo boundaries. Consistent with this approach, the chapter
does not rely on user studies, controlled experiments, benchmark measurements, or
any measured evaluation, and it makes no claim of production readiness; its
results are design artifacts --- an architecture, a schema, an interface design,
and a set of processing flows --- rather than experimental findings.

\section{Requirements Analysis}

This section states the requirements that drive the LUVE design, separating the
functional requirements (what the system must do) from the non-functional and
design requirements (the constraints and properties the design must respect).
The requirements correspond to the controlled-demo baseline \texttt{main@6a61bc8}.

\subsection{Functional Requirements}

At the functional level, the system must support the following capabilities. It
must authenticate a learner and provide current-user lookup, so that practice
sessions are scoped to the authenticated user. It must allow a learner to start
and complete a speaking-practice session. During a session, it must carry the
real-time audio and control flow between the browser and the gateway, convert the
learner's speech to text using the accepted stable recognition mode, and generate
real-time conversational tutor replies from the recognized text. On completion,
it must persist the session durably and allow saved sessions to be retrieved for
review. It must grade completed sessions asynchronously through the message queue
and the grading-worker, writing a grading result when grading succeeds. Finally,
where grading does not proceed --- for example, when eligibility conditions are
not met --- it must record a skipped or unavailable grading state so that such
cases remain observable rather than silently lost.

\subsection{Non-functional Requirements}

The non-functional and design requirements constrain how those capabilities are
realized. The work is scoped as a controlled-demo, research prototype rather than
a production system. The design must separate the real-time hot path from
asynchronous grading, so that the cost of grading does not affect the live
interaction. Durable state must be held in PostgreSQL, and the
completion-to-grading hand-off must be modeled with the \texttt{session\_outbox}
table treated as a durable intent inside the database, not as a standalone queue
service: the session state and the outbox intent are committed together, and an
inline publish of the work item to RabbitMQ is attempted after the database
commit rather than being assumed to succeed. Recovery from a failed publish must
not be assumed automatic, since the relay is disabled by default and is manual
unless explicitly enabled; accordingly, delivery is at-least-once-oriented with
idempotent, session-level processing rather than exactly-once. For privacy, raw
recognized transcript text must be redacted from the relevant STT recognition
log lines, which retain only metadata such as text length and confidence values.
The design must also remain honest about what it does not provide: there is no
production-grade continuous-integration or quality-assurance process, and no
benchmark, user-study, or learning-effectiveness result is claimed. Recognition
is English-focused in the stable demo mode, so stable multilingual recognition is
not claimed; the pronunciation value is a clarity estimate from
speech-recognition confidence and uncertainty rather than acoustic or
phoneme-level scoring; and horizontal scaling is not validated. Finally, the
unmerged ``cat companion'' interface (PR~\#2) is excluded from the baseline and
is not treated as an implemented requirement.

\section{System Design}
\subsection{System Architecture}

LUVE is built as a research prototype for real-time English speaking
practice. The version presented in this thesis corresponds to the frozen
source-code baseline \texttt{main@6a61bc8}. The system is organized as a
multi-service application running under Docker Compose. Client-facing and
control boundaries use HTTP and WebRTC where applicable, PostgreSQL provides
durable persistence, and RabbitMQ carries asynchronous grading work. This
organization cleanly separates
the real-time conversation flow from the asynchronous grading flow, allowing
one component to be developed or replaced without directly affecting the
others.

  the baseline architecture in this thesis.

  \item \textbf{Core API.} A FastAPI service (file \texttt{main.py}, port
  8000) responsible for REST functions such as authentication, current-user
  lookup, session management, and saved-result retrieval. It is the entry point
  for operations that are not part of the real-time audio path.

  \item \textbf{Real-time TEN/WebRTC gateway.} A second FastAPI application
  (file \texttt{run\_ten.py}, port 8080) that shares the same codebase as
  the Core API but serves the real-time conversation path exclusively. This
  gateway establishes the WebRTC~\cite{webrtcSpec} connection with the browser,
  receives the learner's audio stream, and orchestrates the processing steps on
  the ``hot path''. The path is capped at a single session per node
  (\textit{single-session-per-node}).

  \item \textbf{Speech-to-Text (STT) component.} LUVE uses the
  \texttt{faster-whisper} model with the stable demo configuration
  \texttt{small.en} in \texttt{forced\_en} mode (forced English
  recognition), and the second-pass verification step is disabled by
  default. The STT component converts the learner's audio into text together
  with confidence metrics~\cite{radfordWhisper,fasterWhisper}.

  \item \textbf{Tutor response component (LLM tutor).} The recognized text
  is fed into a large language model~\cite{brownGPT3} to generate a conversational
  reply for the learner. In the real-time flow, the large language model is used to
  generate conversational replies appropriate to the context of the
  learner's utterance. This component is not described as the primary grader;
  the full grading/scoring analysis and grading results are processed
  asynchronously by the grading-worker after the session ends. This component
  provides the ``tutor'' behavior of the system.

  \item \textbf{PostgreSQL database.} Stores the system's persistent data,
  including users, lessons, practice sessions, and grading results. In
  particular, the database contains the \texttt{session\_outbox} table that
  supports the reliable event-delivery mechanism (described later).

  \item \textbf{RabbitMQ message queue.} Acts as the asynchronous channel
  between the conversation flow and the grading flow. When a session ends, the
  durable \texttt{session\_outbox} intent is recorded and an inline publish of
  the \texttt{session.completed} work item to RabbitMQ is attempted to trigger
  the grading process.

  \item \textbf{Grading service (grading-worker).} A consumer process that
  listens on the \texttt{luve.session.completed} queue. It performs grading
  for the completed session and writes the results back to the database for
  later display.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Main data and control flow.} A typical practice session proceeds as
follows. First, the learner speaks into the microphone in the browser; the
real-time audio stream is transported to the TEN/WebRTC gateway over the
WebRTC connection. There, the STT component converts the audio into
recognized text. This text is passed to the LLM tutor component to generate
a conversational reply appropriate to the context, which is then played back
to the learner. Session events are collected during the interaction and
persisted when the session ends. At session completion, the system writes
the session record and a \texttt{session\_outbox} record within the same
database transaction, after which an inline publish of the
\texttt{session.completed} work item to RabbitMQ is attempted. If the publish
fails, the outbox row remains for manual recovery, since the relay is disabled
by default. If the process crashes before session completion, part of the event
log still held in memory may not have been persisted; this is a limitation of
the current prototype. When the work item reaches the grading flow, the
grading-worker receives it, performs grading, and stores the results in the
database for later display to the learner.

\textbf{Boundary between real-time conversation and asynchronous grading.}
An important design characteristic of LUVE is the clear separation between
the two flows. The real-time conversation flow (between the browser and the
TEN/WebRTC gateway) requires low latency and runs online, whereas the grading
flow is asynchronous and does not sit on the hot path. Grading is triggered
only after the session has ended, through the message queue. Thanks to this
boundary, the latency or computational load of the grading stage does not
affect the learner's conversation experience. The event-delivery mechanism
uses the durable \textit{outbox} pattern~\cite{transactionalOutbox}: the outbox record is written in the
same transaction as the session record, so it is not lost once the
transaction succeeds. However, in the current version the outbox
\textit{relay} is disabled by default, so recovery after a failed event
publish is a manual operation unless the relay is enabled. Message delivery
is \textit{at-least-once}, and the grading process is designed to be
idempotent through deduplication on \texttt{session\_id}; therefore the
system does not claim exactly-once delivery guarantees.

\textbf{Privacy and security notes.} The real-time session control routes,
specifically \texttt{/rtc/offer}, \texttt{/rtc/ice}, and \texttt{/rtc/cmd},
are protected by a session-ownership check: only the legitimate owner of a
session may operate on it. However, not every gateway endpoint is protected:
the health and diagnostic endpoints return only operational metadata and
remain a security hardening limitation. In addition, after the privacy patch
PR~\#1 (merged into \texttt{main}), the log records no longer store the
learner's raw recognized text; instead, the logs keep only metadata such as
text length and confidence metrics. This is an improvement in user-data
protection during experimental operation.

\textbf{Prototype limitation.} It must be emphasized that the architecture
above is designed for a controlled demo serving research purposes, not for
production-scale deployment. The real-time path is capped at a single session
per node, and the system has not demonstrated horizontal scaling. These
limitations are analyzed in more detail in Chapter~5.

\subsection{Database Design}

LUVE uses PostgreSQL as the single durable state store for the system. A
relational database is appropriate here because the application's core
entities --- accounts, practice sessions, and grading outcomes --- have
well-defined relationships and benefit from transactional guarantees. In
particular, the session-completion step must update session state and record
an outbound grading event together as an atomic unit; PostgreSQL's
transactional semantics make this possible within a single commit, which is
the foundation of the reliability design discussed below. The schema is
defined in the baseline initialization script and evolved through a small set
of numbered SQL migration files (no object-relational mapping migration tool
is used).

Figure~\ref{fig:erd} shows the entity-relationship structure of the LUVE
database. The baseline schema (\texttt{main@6a61bc8}) contains six tables,
each with a distinct responsibility:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \texttt{users} --- authenticated account records for learners. This
  table is the identity anchor of the system; other records that belong to a
  user reference it.

  \item \texttt{lessons} --- metadata for lesson or practice content. It acts
  as the catalogue of practice material that a session may be associated with
  in the baseline schema.

  \item \texttt{sessions} --- the lifecycle and metadata of a practice
  session. A session row captures the state of a speaking session and the
  information needed after it ends, including its completion status, a backup
  of the session payload (\texttt{raw\_backup\_json}), and the end timestamp
  (\texttt{ended\_at}). This table is the central record around which the
  grading pipeline is organized.

  \item \texttt{grading\_results} --- the persisted grading output for a
  completed session. It stores the overall and per-skill scores
  (fluency, grammar, vocabulary, and a pronunciation-clarity score) together
  with structured per-skill feedback (\texttt{skill\_feedback\_json}) and
  provenance fields such as the scoring-schema version, the grading provider,
  and the grader version. The pronunciation-clarity score is a clarity
  estimate derived from speech-recognition confidence, not an acoustic or
  phoneme-level pronunciation score.

  \item \texttt{grading\_skip\_log} --- a record of cases in which grading was
  skipped, together with the reason. This captures sessions that did not meet
  the eligibility conditions for grading, so that skipped cases remain
  observable rather than silently disappearing.

  \item \texttt{session\_outbox} --- a durable outbox table that bridges
  session completion and the asynchronous delivery of the grading event. It
  is a regular PostgreSQL table, written within the same transaction as the
  session record, and is \emph{not} a standalone queue service.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Relationships between tables.} The principal relationships follow the
lifecycle of a practice session. In the application flow, sessions belong to
authenticated users; at the schema level, \texttt{sessions.user\_id}
references \texttt{users.id}, while the implementation remains tolerant of
nullable session ownership in some records. Each completed \texttt{sessions}
record is associated with the grading artefacts it produces:
\texttt{grading\_results.session\_id} and \texttt{grading\_skip\_log.session\_id}
are each unique per session in their respective tables. A
\texttt{grading\_results} row holds the scores for a graded session, while a
\texttt{grading\_skip\_log} row can record cases where grading is skipped or
cannot proceed; the schema does not need to be described as enforcing a
cross-table exclusive choice between the two. The asynchronous delivery
boundary is represented by \texttt{session\_outbox}, where a row corresponds
to the \texttt{session.completed} event of a given session. A session may be
tied to specific practice material: \texttt{sessions.lesson\_id} is optional
and references \texttt{lessons.id}; if a lesson is deleted, the session record
can remain with \texttt{lesson\_id} set to \texttt{NULL}. Grading-related rows
are keyed so that the grading process can be deduplicated on the session
identifier.

\textbf{Support for the runtime flow.} The schema mirrors the end-to-end
runtime sequence described in Section~3.3.1. The real-time speaking session
occurs first, on the conversation path; during the interaction, session
events are accumulated. Durable session state is persisted only at completion:
when the session ends, the system writes the \texttt{sessions} row and a
\texttt{session\_outbox} row in one database transaction, and the system then
attempts to deliver the \texttt{session.completed} work item across the queue
boundary.
Later, and off the real-time path, the grading-worker consumes that event and
writes the resulting \texttt{grading\_results} (or a \texttt{grading\_skip\_log}
entry when grading is skipped). The application uses the schema to persist the
main runtime stages in order: live session $\rightarrow$ durable session state
$\rightarrow$ outbox event $\rightarrow$ grading result.

\textbf{Reliability design.} The \texttt{session\_outbox} table is the core of
the reliability boundary between the synchronous session and the asynchronous
grading pipeline. Because the outbox row is committed in the same transaction
as the session record, the intent to grade a completed session is captured
durably even if the subsequent message publish to RabbitMQ fails. In the
current baseline the inline publish is the live delivery path, and the outbox
relay that would automatically re-deliver pending rows is disabled by default;
recovery from a failed publish is therefore a manual operation unless the
relay is enabled. The design is oriented toward at-least-once delivery, and
grading is idempotent through deduplication on the session identifier, so a
re-delivered event does not produce duplicate results. The system does not
provide, and this thesis does not claim, exactly-once delivery.

\textbf{Privacy and design notes.} The persisted session and grading data are
deliberately kept separate from application logs. Following the backend
privacy patch (PR~\#1, merged into \texttt{main}), raw recognized transcript
text is redacted from the relevant STT recognition log lines; those lines retain
metadata such as text length and confidence values. Raw transcript text is thus
not treated as persistent application logging; the durable record of a session
lives in the database tables described above rather than in log output.

\textbf{Limitations.} The schema is created from a baseline initialization
script and evolved through numbered SQL migration files, but the current
baseline has no production-grade automatic migration runner; migrations are
manual SQL files with manual verification and readiness checks. As a
consequence, a database volume that predates a migration may
require manual verification that the migration has been applied before the
corresponding feature behaves correctly. In addition, because durable session
state is written only at completion, a process crash before completion may
lose the portion of the session event log still held in memory. These
constraints are consistent with the controlled-demo, research-prototype scope
of the system and are revisited in Chapter~5.

\subsection{Interface Design}

The interface is the presentation layer of LUVE and the learner's entry point
to the system. It is a frontend client that sits on top of the two backend
surfaces described earlier: the Core API for authentication, session
management, and saved-session review, and the real-time gateway for the
WebRTC audio and control flow of a live session. The interface does not
implement application logic of its own beyond presentation and orchestration;
it exposes system capabilities by calling the backend contracts and rendering
their responses. This section describes the baseline client (\texttt{main@6a61bc8})
at the design level; the unmerged ``cat companion'' interface (PR~\#2) is not
part of the baseline and is not treated as an implemented feature here.

\textbf{Authentication and session ownership.} The interface distinguishes an
unauthenticated state from an authenticated one. Unauthenticated users are
limited to the introductory and authentication entry points, including
sign-in and account creation, while the practice and review screens are
available only after authentication. Once signed in, the client issues
user-scoped API calls and operates within the backend's session-ownership
model, so that a learner can act only on their own sessions; the real-time
control routes are likewise guarded by the same ownership check on the gateway.
The interface therefore mirrors, at the presentation layer, the access
boundaries that the backend enforces.

\subsection{Algorithm and Processing Flow Design}

Rather than a single monolithic algorithm, LUVE is organized as a set of
cooperating processing flows that together realize an end-to-end speaking
practice session. This section describes those flows in terms of data flow,
control flow, and the boundaries between components. Five flows are presented:
the real-time speaking flow, the session lifecycle flow, the outbox and queue
flow, the asynchronous grading flow, and the retrieval/display flow. The
design deliberately keeps the latency-sensitive real-time work separate from
the deferred grading work, and the transitions between the two are mediated by
durable database state.

\textbf{Real-time speaking flow.} The real-time flow runs on the conversation
path between the browser and the TEN/WebRTC gateway. The browser captures the
learner's audio and exchanges audio and control messages with the gateway over
the WebRTC connection. The gateway manages the state of the active real-time
session and, where applicable, applies voice-activity detection (VAD) and
audio chunking to identify speech segments. Each segment is passed to the
speech-to-text (STT) component, which in the stable demo configuration runs in
\texttt{forced\_en} mode with the \texttt{small.en} model and second-pass
verification disabled, producing recognized text together with confidence
metrics. The recognized text is forwarded to the real-time LLM tutor, which
generates conversational replies only; it is not the grading component, and no
full scoring or grading/scoring analysis is produced on this path. Tutor
responses are returned to the learner through the TTS/WebRTC output path when
tutor audio is enabled. For privacy, following the backend privacy patch
(PR~\#1), raw recognized transcript text is redacted from the relevant STT
recognition log lines; those lines retain metadata such as text length and
confidence values.

\textbf{Session lifecycle flow.} A practice session is created through the
Core API and, in the application flow, is associated with an authenticated
user. While the session is active, the real-time events it produces are
collected during the interaction. Durable persistence happens at completion:
when the session ends, the system records the session state and the
information needed for downstream processing. Because events are accumulated in
memory during the interaction and persisted only at completion, a process
crash before completion may lose the portion of the event log not yet written;
this is an accepted limitation of the current prototype. Completion is also the
point at which downstream grading work is created and scheduled, which links
this flow to the outbox and queue flow below.

\textbf{Outbox and queue flow.} The hand-off from a completed session to the
grading pipeline is built on a durable outbox. The \texttt{session\_outbox}
table is an ordinary PostgreSQL table, not a standalone queue service. At
completion, the session state update and the corresponding outbox row are
committed together in a single database transaction, so the intent to grade is
recorded durably and atomically with the session itself. After the commit, the
system attempts an inline publish of the \texttt{session.completed} event to
RabbitMQ. If the publish fails, a warning is logged and the durable outbox row
remains in place for later recovery. The outbox relay that would automatically
re-deliver pending rows is disabled by default, so recovery after a failed
publish is a manual operation unless the relay is enabled. The mechanism is
oriented toward at-least-once delivery rather than exactly-once; to keep this
safe, downstream processing is idempotent and deduplicated at the session
level, so a re-delivered event does not produce duplicate results.

\textbf{Asynchronous grading flow.} Grading runs off the real-time path. The
grading-worker consumes the \texttt{session.completed} work item when it is
received from the queue, reads the completed session and its transcript
evidence, and
produces a grading outcome. When grading succeeds, it writes a
\texttt{grading\_results} row; when grading cannot proceed or is intentionally
skipped (for example, when eligibility conditions are not met), it records a
\texttt{grading\_skip\_log} entry instead. The system supports two grading
modes: an offline \emph{fake} (development-preview) grader, which is the
default, and a real LLM-based grader that can be enabled by configuration.
Among the reported skills, the pronunciation score is a clarity estimate
derived from STT confidence and uncertainty signals, not an acoustic or
phoneme-level pronunciation analysis.

\textbf{Retrieval and display flow.} Grading is not synchronous with the
conversation, so results become available after the session ends. The client
later retrieves saved sessions and their grading results from the Core API for
display to the learner. The detailed user-interface design is covered
separately in the Interface Design section; here it is sufficient to note that
the retrieval path reads persisted records rather than participating in the
real-time pipeline.

\textbf{Failure and limitation notes.} The processing flows above operate
within the controlled-demo, research-prototype scope of the system. Several
constraints follow from this scope and shape the design. Schema changes rely on
numbered manual SQL migration files with manual verification; there is no
production-grade automatic migration runner. Readiness and observability are
limited --- health checks are shallow and there is no dedicated metrics
endpoint --- so operational state is inferred largely from logs. The real-time
path is capped at a single session per node, and horizontal scaling is not
claimed or demonstrated. The STT configuration is English-focused
(\texttt{forced\_en} with \texttt{small.en}); stable multilingual recognition
is not claimed. Likewise, the system does not claim exactly-once delivery or
acoustic/phoneme-level pronunciation scoring. These limitations are revisited
in Chapter~5.

\section{Chapter Summary}

This chapter presented the system design of LUVE across four views. The
system architecture (Section~3.3.1) organizes the system as cooperating
components --- a browser client, the Core API, the TEN/WebRTC real-time
gateway, the STT component, the real-time LLM tutor that produces
conversational replies, a PostgreSQL database, a RabbitMQ message queue, and
the grading-worker. Client-facing and control boundaries use HTTP and WebRTC
where applicable; PostgreSQL provides durable persistence; and RabbitMQ carries
asynchronous grading work. The
database design (Section~3.3.2) organizes durable state around six tables ---
\texttt{users}, \texttt{lessons}, \texttt{sessions}, \texttt{grading\_results},
\texttt{grading\_skip\_log}, and \texttt{session\_outbox} --- where the outbox
table is the durable bridge between session completion and asynchronous
grading. The interface design (Section~3.3.3) treats the frontend as a
presentation and orchestration layer over the Core API and the real-time
gateway, rather than a holder of application logic. The processing-flow design
(Section~3.3.4) ties these together: the real-time speaking flow runs on the
conversation path, durable session state is persisted at completion, the
\texttt{session.completed} event is committed to \texttt{session\_outbox}, after
which an inline RabbitMQ publish is attempted, and grading runs asynchronously
off the hot path.

These design decisions are bounded by explicit limitations that frame the scope
of the work. LUVE is a controlled-demo, research-prototype system and is not
production-ready. The stable demo STT mode is \texttt{forced\_en} with the
\texttt{small.en} model and second-pass verification disabled. The reported
pronunciation value is a clarity estimate derived from speech-recognition
confidence and uncertainty, not acoustic or phoneme-level scoring.
\texttt{session\_outbox} is a durable PostgreSQL bridge whose relay is disabled
by default, so recovery from a failed publish is manual unless the relay is
enabled; message delivery is at-least-once with idempotent grading rather than
exactly-once. Horizontal scaling is not claimed. These limitations are
revisited in Chapter~5.

\section{Implementation Process}
\subsection{Main Module Implementation}

This section describes how the main modules of LUVE are implemented and how
they cooperate to make the system run end to end. The description is at the
system level: it focuses on each module's responsibility and its boundaries
with the others rather than on line-level code. All modules correspond to the
frozen baseline \texttt{main@6a61bc8}.

\textbf{Client module.} In the repository, this module is the Vite client under
\texttt{client/}, with package metadata in \texttt{client/package.json}; during
the Core API image build, the frontend is compiled and copied into the Core API
runtime image as static assets. The client is a single-page application built
with React, Vite, and TypeScript. Instead of being deployed as a separate
frontend service, it is built into static assets and served by the Core API
runtime. Its responsibilities are confined to the presentation layer: it
provides the authentication entry point, the controls to start and manage a
practice session, the real-time interaction surface during a speaking session,
and the review of saved sessions and their grading results. The unmerged ``cat
companion'' interface (PR~\#2) is not part of the baseline client and is not
treated as an implemented module here.

\textbf{Core API module.} The Core API is implemented in Python with the
FastAPI framework. The implementation anchor is
\texttt{services/core-api/src/main.py}, and the corresponding Docker Compose
service is \texttt{core\_api}. It exposes the system's REST surface, handling
authentication, current-user lookup, session management, and retrieval of
saved-session results for display in the client; it also serves the static
frontend assets described above. The Core API is the entry point for
client-facing operations that are not on the real-time audio path. With respect
to privacy, for the STT recognition path the PR~\#1 privacy patch redacts raw
recognized transcript text from the relevant recognition log lines; those log
lines retain metadata such as text length and confidence values instead. The
module's REST responsibilities are deliberately scoped to the endpoints above
and do not constitute a broad user-management subsystem.

\textbf{TEN/WebRTC gateway module.} The real-time gateway is a second FastAPI
application that runs from the same \texttt{core-api} codebase but serves the
live conversation path exclusively. The implementation anchor is
\texttt{services/core-api/run\_ten.py}; in Docker Compose it runs as the
\texttt{ten\_gateway} service. It performs WebRTC negotiation and carries the
audio and control messages of a speaking session, maintaining the state of the
active real-time session. The gateway is the point at which the real-time flow
is connected to the STT module and the LLM tutor reply path: it routes the
learner's audio to recognition and returns the tutor's conversational responses.
Its session-control routes --- \texttt{/rtc/offer}, \texttt{/rtc/ice}, and
\texttt{/rtc/cmd} --- are protected by a session-ownership check; not every
gateway endpoint is protected, however: the health and diagnostic endpoints
expose only operational metadata and remain a security-hardening caveat rather
than a fully protected surface.

\textbf{STT module.} Speech recognition is implemented with the
\texttt{faster-whisper} implementation of Whisper. In the stable demo
configuration it uses the \texttt{small.en} model in \texttt{forced\_en} mode
with second-pass verification disabled, so the recognition path is
English-focused; stable multilingual recognition is not claimed. The module
produces the recognized transcript together with confidence and uncertainty
signals that later inform grading. These signals are recognition-level
indicators only; the module does not perform acoustic or phoneme-level
pronunciation scoring.

\textbf{Real-time LLM tutor module.} The tutor module passes the recognized
text to a large language model to generate conversational replies during the
live interaction. Its role is strictly conversational: it keeps the exchange
going and returns context-appropriate responses, but it does not produce full
grading or scoring. This responsibility boundary is important to the design:
instead of performing grading in the real-time gateway, the baseline delegates
scoring to the worker after session completion, so the real-time tutor and the
grading-worker are distinct modules and all grading/scoring is deferred to the
asynchronous grading stage rather than computed on the real-time path.

\textbf{Persistence and queue module.} Durable state is held in a PostgreSQL
database, which stores the six baseline tables: \texttt{users}, \texttt{lessons},
\texttt{sessions}, \texttt{grading\_results}, \texttt{grading\_skip\_log}, and
\texttt{session\_outbox}. RabbitMQ is the asynchronous message broker that
carries the \texttt{session.completed} work to the grading stage. The
\texttt{session\_outbox} table is the bridge between the two: at session
completion the session state and the outbox row are committed together in one
database transaction, after which an inline publish of the
\texttt{session.completed} work item to RabbitMQ is attempted. This
outbox write is an implementation choice to reduce the dual-write failure risk
between committing session state in PostgreSQL and publishing the RabbitMQ work
item. Because the durable intent to grade is captured atomically with the
session, failed delivery can be recovered from the outbox; in the baseline,
however, the relay is disabled by default, so recovery is manual unless the
relay is explicitly enabled. The mechanism is oriented toward at-least-once
delivery with idempotent, session-level deduplication rather than exactly-once
delivery.

\textbf{Grading-worker module.} The grading-worker is a separate consumer
process that listens for \texttt{session.completed} work. The implementation
anchor is \texttt{services/grading-worker/src/worker.py}; in Docker Compose it
runs as \texttt{grading\_worker}. For each completed session it reads the
session and its transcript evidence and produces an outcome: on success it
writes a \texttt{grading\_results} row, and when grading cannot proceed or is
intentionally skipped it records a \texttt{grading\_skip\_log} entry instead. The worker supports two grading modes --- an offline
development-preview (``fake'') grader, which is the default, and an optional
real LLM-based grader enabled by configuration. Among the reported skills, the
pronunciation value is a clarity estimate derived from speech-recognition
confidence and uncertainty, not an acoustic or phoneme-level pronunciation
score.

\textbf{Runtime and deployment integration.} The modules above are coordinated
for local and demonstration use through the Docker Compose app profile, which
brings up the infrastructure services, the Core API, the TEN/WebRTC gateway,
and the grading-worker. The default configuration runs on CPU, with an opt-in
GPU configuration available for the STT gateway where suitable hardware is
present, as described in Section~4.1. Consistent with the prototype scope, this
integration targets a controlled-demo deployment: it is not production-ready,
and no horizontal-scaling claim is made --- the real-time path is operated at
the scale of a single session per node.

\subsection{Data Processing}

This section follows the data as it moves through LUVE's implemented runtime
pipeline, from the learner's spoken input to the stored grading result and its
later retrieval. The description traces the transformations performed at each
stage and the module responsible for them, staying within the controlled-demo
baseline \texttt{main@6a61bc8}.

\textbf{Real-time audio-to-text processing.} Data enters the pipeline as audio
captured in the browser. The TEN/WebRTC gateway receives the real-time audio
and control flow and forwards the audio to the STT module. In the stable demo
configuration, recognition runs with the \texttt{small.en} model in
\texttt{forced\_en} mode and second-pass verification disabled, so the
recognition path is English-focused and stable multilingual recognition is not
claimed. The STT module produces recognized text together with
confidence/uncertainty-style metadata where available. These signals are
recognition-level indicators and are not an acoustic or phoneme-level
pronunciation analysis. For privacy, after the PR~\#1 patch the raw recognized
transcript text is not written to the STT recognition log lines; those lines
retain metadata such as text length and confidence values instead.

\textbf{Real-time reply processing.} The recognized text is passed into the
real-time LLM tutor path, which transforms it into a conversational reply.
At this stage the tutor produces conversational replies only; full
grading/scoring is not performed on the real-time path. When tutor audio is
enabled, the reply is returned to the learner through the TTS/WebRTC output
path. The data handled here is therefore transient conversational state rather
than any persisted assessment.

\textbf{Session event processing.} While a session is active, its events are
collected during the interaction. The session record exists in PostgreSQL, but
the real-time event log and transcript evidence are accumulated in memory during
the interaction and are persisted or updated at session completion rather than
continuously. A consequence of this design is that if the real-time process
crashes before completion, the in-memory event log or transcript evidence may be
lost even though the broader session record can already exist. At completion,
the system persists the session state and, in the same step, creates the
\texttt{session\_outbox} intent that represents the work to be graded.

\textbf{Completion, outbox, and queue processing.} The transition from a
completed session to the grading pipeline is the central data hand-off.
\texttt{session\_outbox} is a PostgreSQL table, not a queue service. At
completion the session state and the outbox intent are committed together in a
single database transaction, after which an inline publish of the
\texttt{session.completed} work item to RabbitMQ is attempted. If the publish
fails, a warning is logged and the durable outbox row remains so the work is not
lost. The relay that would re-deliver pending outbox rows is disabled by
default, so recovery from a failed publish is manual unless the relay is
explicitly enabled. As a result, delivery of the work item is oriented toward
at-least-once behavior with idempotent, session-level deduplication, rather than
exactly-once delivery.

\textbf{Asynchronous grading data processing.} Off the real-time path, the
grading-worker consumes the \texttt{session.completed} work item. It reads the
completed session and its transcript evidence and transforms that evidence into
a grading outcome. On success it writes a \texttt{grading\_results} row; when
grading cannot proceed or is intentionally skipped, it writes a
\texttt{grading\_skip\_log} row instead. Two grading modes are distinguished: an
offline development-preview (``fake'') grader, which is the default, and an
optional real LLM-based grader enabled by configuration. Among the reported
skills, the pronunciation value is interpreted as a clarity estimate derived
from STT confidence and uncertainty, not a phoneme-level or acoustic
pronunciation score.

\textbf{Saved-session retrieval.} After completion, and after grading when
available, the data is read back on demand. The client retrieves saved sessions
and their grading results through
the Core API and renders them for review. Because grading is asynchronous, the
displayed result reflects the backend state of a session: at the design level
the interface can show pending or processing grading, an available graded
result, an insufficient-evidence or skipped outcome, and a failed or
unavailable analysis, to the extent these states are represented by the
backend. The ``cat companion'' interface (PR~\#2) is not part of the baseline
and is not used as the basis for this retrieval view.

\section{Testing}

The verification activities described in this section are appropriate to a
controlled-demo, research prototype rather than to production-grade quality
assurance. Their purpose is to check whether the implemented system flows behave
as designed --- recognition, tutor replies, session persistence, the grading
hand-off, and result retrieval --- and not to measure educational impact or to
certify production readiness. The discussion therefore emphasizes
\emph{verification} and \emph{smoke testing}, and it distinguishes checks that
have been used during development from checks that are recommended as a
pre-demo verification checklist.

\textbf{Static and build verification.} During development, static and build
checks were used to catch integration regressions early. On the backend, Python
compile and import smoke checks confirm that the application modules load, and
on the frontend the TypeScript and Vite build steps confirm that the client
compiles into the static assets served by the Core API. These checks are used
locally; the project does not operate a continuous-integration pipeline, and no
automated build-gate results are claimed here.

\textbf{Backend and focused tests.} Backend verification relies on targeted
tests and inspections of selected behaviors rather than on a single exhaustive
suite. Focused tests are run per service using each service's \texttt{pytest}
setup, exercising specific logic such as the grading-worker's handling of a
completed session and the persistence/outbox path that records the intent to
grade. These are intentionally narrow checks of particular behaviors; this
thesis does not report test counts, pass rates, or coverage figures, as such
figures are not part of the verified baseline.

\textbf{Runtime smoke testing and pre-demo verification checklist.} Because LUVE
is exercised as a live demo, the most informative verification is at the runtime
level. The following sequence is used as a pre-demo verification checklist
rather than as a source of quantitative results. First, the controlled local
stack is started through the Docker Compose app profile, and service readiness
is checked: the Core API readiness endpoint confirms database reachability and
the gateway readiness endpoint confirms that the real-time gateway has
initialized. PostgreSQL and RabbitMQ availability is confirmed, since both are
required for the end-to-end flow. A browser-based real-time speaking session is
then initiated through the TEN/WebRTC gateway, and the STT output is checked to
confirm that recognized speech is processed while the relevant STT recognition
log lines retain only metadata, with the raw transcript text redacted as
established by the privacy patch. A session is then completed, and the
\texttt{session.completed} hand-off is checked together with the
\texttt{session\_outbox} behavior. Finally, the saved sessions and any grading
results are retrieved through the Core API to confirm that the review path
works. Where these steps have not been executed in a given environment, they
remain a recommended checklist rather than a recorded test result.

\textbf{Negative and failure-path verification.} Beyond the happy path, a few

There is no production-grade continuous-integration pipeline and no automated
test gate. No load or stress testing has been performed, and horizontal scaling
has not been validated, consistent with the single-node, controlled-demo scope.
No user study or learning-effectiveness measurement has been conducted, and no
true acoustic or phoneme-level pronunciation validation has been performed, in
line with the system's clarity-estimate treatment of pronunciation. Stable
multilingual recognition has likewise not been validated, since the demo mode is
English-focused. Finally, the full test suite has intermittently hung in some

\chapter{CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK}

\section{Conclusion}

This thesis designed and implemented LUVE, a real-time English speaking
practice system, as a controlled-demo, research prototype rather than a
production-ready product. The work was approached from a software and
system-design perspective, and its contribution lies in the architecture, the
module boundaries, the data and control flows, and the runtime integration that
together let the prototype run end to end. The frozen baseline
\texttt{main@6a61bc8} served as the single reference point for every design and
implementation claim in the preceding chapters.

At the system-design level, LUVE integrates a frontend client with the Core API
and the TEN/WebRTC gateway. The client is a React/Vite/TypeScript single-page
application that is built into static assets and served by the Core API rather
than deployed as a separate service. The Core API provides the REST surface for
authentication, current-user lookup, session management, and saved-result
retrieval, and it also serves the static frontend. The TEN/WebRTC gateway
provides the real-time speaking interaction, carrying the audio and control
flow of a session. Speech recognition runs in the stable demo mode --- the
\texttt{small.en} model in \texttt{forced\_en} mode with second-pass
verification disabled --- and the real-time LLM tutor generates conversational
replies that are deliberately kept separate from grading. Durable state is held
in PostgreSQL, RabbitMQ carries the asynchronous grading work, and the
\texttt{session\_outbox} table acts as a durable PostgreSQL bridge between
session completion and grading; the grading-worker consumes the completed-session
work and produces a \texttt{grading\_results} row on success or a
\texttt{grading\_skip\_log} row when grading cannot proceed.

On the implementation and verification side, the prototype achieves a working,
demo-visible real-time speaking flow and a saved-session and grading flow. It
incorporates a scoped privacy improvement --- the PR~\#1 patch redacts raw STT
transcript text from the relevant recognition log lines --- and a reliability
mechanism in which the outbox preserves the durable intent to grade and a
warning is logged if the inline RabbitMQ publish fails. The verification posture
was defined at a level appropriate to a controlled demo, combining build and
static checks, targeted backend checks, and a runtime smoke/pre-demo checklist;
checklist items are treated as recorded results only when executed in the target
environment.

These results are bounded by limitations that frame the prototype honestly. The
system is not production-ready, and no production-grade continuous-integration or
quality-assurance process is claimed. No learning-effectiveness or user-study
result is reported, and no benchmark, latency, throughput, or coverage figures
are claimed. Stable multilingual recognition is not validated, and the
pronunciation value is a clarity estimate derived from speech-recognition
confidence and uncertainty rather than acoustic or phoneme-level scoring. The
outbox relay is disabled by default, so recovery from a failed publish is manual
unless the relay is explicitly enabled, and delivery is at-least-once-oriented
with idempotent, session-level deduplication rather than exactly-once. Finally,
horizontal scaling is not validated, consistent with the single-node,
controlled-demo scope. In summary, LUVE demonstrates a coherent end-to-end
software design for real-time speaking practice within a clearly bounded
prototype scope, and the directions for extending it are discussed in the
remainder of this chapter.

\section{Thesis Contributions}

The contributions of this thesis are technical and system-oriented. They concern
how LUVE is structured as software and how its runtime concerns are separated,
rather than any educational outcome. The main contributions are summarized
below.

\textbf{System architecture.} The thesis designs a multi-surface architecture
for LUVE with explicit component boundaries: a frontend client; a Core API that
provides the REST surface and serves the static frontend; a TEN/WebRTC gateway
that handles the real-time path; an STT processing module; a real-time LLM tutor
reply path; PostgreSQL for durable persistence; RabbitMQ for asynchronous
grading work; and a grading-worker. The contribution is the way these surfaces
are delineated and connected --- the data and control flows between them and the
boundaries they respect --- so that each component has a single, well-defined
responsibility and can be reasoned about or replaced independently.

\textbf{Real-time and asynchronous separation.} A second contribution is the
clean separation between the real-time speaking interaction and post-session
grading. On the real-time path, the system generates conversational tutor
replies to sustain the interaction; grading and scoring are delegated to the
grading-worker after a session completes. This keeps the latency-sensitive hot
path conceptually and operationally distinct from the slower analysis stage, so
that the cost of grading does not intrude on the live conversation.

\textbf{Persistence and outbox reliability.} The thesis models the transition
from session completion to grading through PostgreSQL together with a
\texttt{session\_outbox} table. The \texttt{session\_outbox} is an ordinary
PostgreSQL table rather than a queue service; it is written in the same
transaction as the session state so that the durable intent to grade survives a
failed RabbitMQ publish. The recovery posture is documented honestly: the relay
that would re-deliver pending rows is disabled by default, so recovery is manual
unless it is explicitly enabled, and the mechanism is at-least-once-oriented with
idempotent, session-level deduplication rather than exactly-once delivery.

\textbf{Truthful AI and scoring boundary.} A further contribution is documenting,
rather than overstating, where artificial intelligence is used. The thesis
distinguishes the real-time LLM tutor replies from the grading stage, and within
grading it distinguishes the default offline development-preview (``fake'')
grader from the optional real LLM-based grader enabled by configuration. It also
frames the pronunciation value as a clarity estimate derived from
speech-recognition confidence and uncertainty, not an acoustic or phoneme-level
score, and it makes no claim of stable multilingual recognition, since the demo
mode is English-focused.

\textbf{DevOps and demo readiness.} Finally, the thesis contributes a coherent
controlled-demo packaging of the system. The services are coordinated for local
use through the Docker Compose app profile, with a CPU-default configuration and
an optional GPU path for the STT gateway as described in Chapter~4. The baseline
also includes the scoped PR~\#1 privacy improvement, which redacts raw STT
transcript text from the relevant recognition log lines, and a verification
posture suited to a controlled demo. Throughout, the limitations of the
prototype are stated explicitly rather than glossed over.

Taken together, these contributions constitute a coherent end-to-end software
design for a real-time speaking-practice prototype, with clearly separated
runtime concerns and an honestly bounded scope. They are deliberately framed as
system-design results: the thesis does not claim production readiness, learning
effectiveness, user-study outcomes, or measured performance, and the
corresponding limitations are detailed in the next section.

\section{Limitations}

This section states the limitations of LUVE honestly and does not soften any
caveat established in the previous chapters. The limitations span the
prototype's scope, its runtime behavior, recognition and grading, persistence,
evaluation, and the baseline user interface.

\textbf{Prototype and deployment scope.} LUVE is a controlled-demo, research
prototype and is not production-ready. There is no production-grade
continuous-integration or quality-assurance process, and no claim to one is
made. Schema evolution relies on numbered manual SQL migrations with manual
verification; there is no production-grade automatic migration runner or
preflight check, so the persisted-data layout depends on those migrations having
been applied in a given environment.

\textbf{Real-time and runtime limitations.} The system is single-node and
controlled-demo oriented, and horizontal scaling has not been validated. Health,
readiness, and observability remain limited: the Core API readiness check covers
database reachability and the gateway readiness check covers startup state, but
RabbitMQ is not fully covered by these shallow checks. No load or stress testing
has been performed, so the prototype's behavior under sustained or concurrent
load is not characterized.

\textbf{STT and pronunciation limitations.} The stable demo recognition mode is
English-focused --- the \texttt{small.en} model in \texttt{forced\_en} mode with
second-pass verification disabled --- and stable multilingual recognition is
neither validated nor claimed. The pronunciation value reported by the system is
a clarity estimate derived from speech-recognition confidence and uncertainty,
not an acoustic or phoneme-level pronunciation score; no true acoustic or
phoneme-level pronunciation validation has been carried out.

\textbf{Persistence and outbox limitations.} The \texttt{session\_outbox} table
captures a durable grading intent in PostgreSQL; it is not a queue service. The
inline publish of the work item to RabbitMQ can fail, in which case the outbox
row remains for recovery. That recovery is not automatic by default: the relay
is disabled unless explicitly enabled, so recovery is otherwise manual. Delivery
is therefore at-least-once-oriented with idempotent, session-level
deduplication, not exactly-once. In addition, the real-time event log and
transcript evidence are accumulated in memory during a session and persisted at
completion, so if the real-time process crashes before completion this in-memory
evidence may be lost even though a broader session record can already exist.

\textbf{AI and grading limitations.} Depending on configuration, grading may run
in an offline development-preview (``fake'') mode, which is the default; the
optional real LLM-based grader is configurable but does not constitute a
production assessment guarantee. No benchmark, accuracy, or grading-validity
result is claimed for either mode. Because grading consumes the completed
session's transcript evidence, the quality of a saved-session grading outcome
depends on the quality of that STT-derived evidence.

\textbf{Evaluation limitations.} The thesis reports no user study and no
learning-effectiveness result. It also reports no benchmark, latency,
throughput, coverage, accuracy, score, test-count, or pass-rate figures. The
runtime smoke and pre-demo checklist is treated as a recommended verification
procedure; its items count as recorded results only when they are actually
executed in the target environment.

\textbf{User-interface baseline limitation.} The ``cat companion'' interface
introduced in PR~\#2 is not part of the baseline and is not described as
implemented baseline functionality; the results and discussion in this thesis
refer only to the baseline client.

\section{Future Work}

The future-work directions below follow directly from the limitations in
Section~5.3. They describe engineering and research directions that a later
version of LUVE could pursue; none of them is implemented in the current
baseline, and they are stated as possibilities rather than as completed results.

\textbf{Production deployment hardening.} If the project were to move beyond a
controlled demo, a later version should add a production-grade migration runner
with a preflight check, so that schema state no longer depends on manual
verification. Readiness checking should be strengthened to cover RabbitMQ and
other dependencies with deeper probes rather than the current shallow checks.
Observability could be extended beyond logs and a dead-letter queue with metrics
and tracing, and continuous-integration and test gates could be introduced once
the system targets more than a controlled demo. These are prerequisites for any
production claim and are explicitly not part of the present prototype.

\textbf{Outbox and queue recovery.} The durable \texttt{session\_outbox} already
captures grading intent, but recovery from a failed publish is manual in the
baseline because the relay is disabled by default. Future work should
operationalize or enable the outbox relay and define explicit retry and
reconciliation procedures for pending rows. Such work should remain
at-least-once-oriented with idempotent processing; it would not, and should not,
be presented as providing exactly-once delivery, and automatic recovery should
not be assumed to exist in the baseline.

\textbf{Real-time reliability and scaling.} A later version could add better
timeout and resource controls around STT and real-time inference, so that a slow
or stuck recognition step does not degrade the session. To reduce the risk that
in-memory event or transcript evidence is lost on a crash before completion,
future work could introduce mid-session durable checkpointing of that evidence.
Multi-session and multi-node scaling could also be investigated; however, this
remains future work, and horizontal scaling should not be described as validated
until it is actually demonstrated.

\textbf{STT and pronunciation improvements.} The baseline deliberately runs an
English-focused recognition mode. Future work may evaluate multilingual or
second-pass STT, but only behind a proper canary rollout and evaluation rather
than by default. Separately, future work may explore true acoustic or
phoneme-level pronunciation scoring as a replacement for the current
confidence-based clarity estimate. Both directions are explicitly not baseline
capabilities, and a later version should validate them before relying on them.

\textbf{AI grading and evaluation.} The grading stage would benefit from a
stronger evaluation methodology for grading quality. Future work could evaluate
the optional real LLM-based grader using validated prompts and curated test
cases, while keeping the distinction between the default offline
development-preview (``fake'') grader and the optional real grader explicit. Any
benchmark or user-study evaluation of grading quality should be treated as future
research; it is not a current result of this thesis.

\textbf{User interface and user-facing refinement.} Future user-interface work
could polish the saved-session review states --- for example, the presentation
of pending, available, and skipped grading outcomes --- and could revisit
optional companion-interface work. The ``cat companion'' interface from PR~\#2
remains outside the thesis baseline and should not be described as baseline
functionality unless it is later merged and re-reviewed.

\textbf{Research evaluation.} Finally, future user studies could evaluate the
usability of the system or, with an appropriate experimental design, learning
outcomes. It should be stated clearly that no such user study and no
learning-effectiveness result is part of the current thesis; these remain
directions for future research rather than findings reported here.

Taken together, these directions would extend LUVE from a coherent controlled-demo
prototype toward a more robust and more thoroughly evaluated system, while
preserving the honest scoping that characterizes the present work.
